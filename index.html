In April 1990, a plan by the NIE and DOE initiated the Human Genome Project, aiming to map and sequence the entire human genome within fifteen years. This effort involved international researchers and set the foundation for nearly fifteen years of continuous research towards completing the project.
In March 1999, the transition from the projects pilot phase changed to full scale sequencing of the entire human genome. Scientists started to slowly unravel mysteries without our DNA, leading to groundbreaking discoveries in genetics and medicine.
In 2003, the Human Genome Project had been completed, achieving its goal of sequencing the entire human genome by 2004. This event provided detailed understanding of human genetics. The International Human Genome Sequence was released on October 20, 2004. After the release, researchers started focusing on analyzing genome data.
In 1995, the Human Genome Project achieved significant milestones, including ones such as the completion of the first map of human chromosome 21. Collaborative efforts among international researchers continued to propel the project, with the publication of a physical map of the human genome laying the groundwork for further assembly.
In 2001, the project had reached a milestone after the first draft's publication of the human genome sequence by the International Human Genome Consortium. This publication corrected previous estimates and highlighted that human genomes are 99.9% identical. This achievement reflected the collaborative efforts of researchers and propelled the project towards completion.
